The Field Service Technician Retention Crisis: What the Data Says

JP
James Park VP of Partnerships
March 3, 2026 9 min read

Last September, I sat across from a master plumber named Derek in a diner outside of Pittsburgh. He had 19 years in the trade, a journeyman and master license, EPA 608 certification, and the kind of diagnostic instinct you only develop after fixing thousands of systems. He told me he was quitting to drive for UPS.

"It is not the money, exactly," he said. "I could make more plumbing than driving. But at UPS I clock in, do my route, clock out, and nobody takes a cut. Nobody sends me across town for a $50 diagnostic when I have a $2,000 repipe on the other side of the city. And the app works."

Derek is not a data point. He is the data point. Multiplied by hundreds of thousands of skilled technicians making the same calculation across every trade in the country.

67% Annual Turnover: The Number That Should Terrify You

The Service Council's 2025 workforce report put field service technician turnover at 67% annually across HVAC, plumbing, electrical, and general maintenance. That means if you employ 30 technicians on January 1, roughly 20 of them will be gone by December 31.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects a shortfall of 238,000 skilled trade workers by 2028. But framing this as a "labor shortage" misses the point. Trade schools are producing graduates. Apprenticeship programs are expanding. The pipeline exists. The problem is the drain at the other end: trained, experienced technicians leaving the trade faster than new ones enter it.

What does that churn actually cost?

  • Recruiting: $800 to $1,500 per hire (job boards, background checks, drug screening)
  • Onboarding: $1,200 to $2,500 (uniform, tools, vehicle setup, platform training)
  • Productivity ramp: $2,000 to $3,500 (a new technician runs at 60-70% efficiency for the first 90 days)
  • Lost revenue during the gap: Variable, but a single unfilled position costs an average of $4,800/month in missed job capacity

Total replacement cost per technician: $4,000 to $7,500, depending on trade and market. For a 50-person operation running at the industry-average 67% turnover, that is $134,000 to $251,000 per year spent on replacing people who already knew how to do the job. Every year. On repeat.

The Three Root Causes (And One Nobody Talks About)

1. The Pay Gap Between Gross and Net

I have connected over 5,000 technicians to field service platforms over the past four years. When I ask why they left their previous platform, the answer is almost never "the pay was too low." The answer is "the pay was too low after the platform took its cut."

The distinction matters. A technician billing $65/hour through a platform with a 22% take rate is earning $50.70 effective. After fuel ($180/week average), vehicle wear ($120/week), tool replacement ($40/week), and self-employment taxes on 1099 income (15.3% SE tax), the real hourly rate drops into the low $30s.

Meanwhile, an Amazon warehouse worker in the same metro area starts at $21/hour with health insurance, 401(k) matching, and a schedule posted two weeks in advance. A UPS driver makes $35 to $42/hour with full Teamster benefits after seniority. Neither job requires a trade license, a personal vehicle, or $15,000 in tools.

The Mechanical Contractors Association surveyed technicians who left the trade in 2025. 41% cited "inadequate compensation after platform and overhead deductions" as their primary reason. Not low hourly rates. The gap between what the customer paid and what they actually kept.

2. Professional Disrespect Baked Into the Platform

This is the cause that does not show up in compensation surveys but drives more departures than anyone wants to admit.

Field service technicians hold trade licenses that require 4 to 5 years of apprenticeship, hundreds of hours of classroom instruction, and proctored examinations. Master electricians, master plumbers, NATE-certified HVAC technicians: these are skilled professionals with credentials that took years to earn.

Then they log into a platform that treats them like Uber drivers.

Rigid scheduling algorithms assign jobs without considering a technician's specialization. A commercial refrigeration expert gets routed to a residential thermostat install. A master electrician with 200-amp panel experience gets sent to replace a GFCI outlet. The platform's algorithm does not know or care about the difference. It sees an available body and an open job.

Customer rating systems designed for ride-sharing get applied to complex technical work. A single 3-star review, left by a homeowner frustrated about the cost of the repair (not the quality of the work), dings the technician's standing. In focus groups conducted by Field Service News in late 2025, the phrase that came up most was "treated like a number."

"I spent five years getting my master plumber's license. The platform app does not even have a field for my license number. But it has a field for my customer rating. That tells you everything about how they see us." - Focus group participant, Field Service News, October 2025

3. Tools That Fight You Instead of Helping

Technicians spend an average of 47 minutes per day on administrative work that contributes nothing to their earnings or the quality of their service. Paper forms that duplicate digital entries. Photo uploads that fail in basements with no signal. Scheduling apps that do not account for drive time between jobs. Parts inventory systems that show stock as "available" when it is sitting in a warehouse two hours away.

Individually, each friction point is minor. Accumulated across a 10-hour workday, 5 days a week, 50 weeks a year, it is 196 hours of annual frustration. That is nearly 5 full work weeks spent fighting software that was designed by engineers who have never crawled through an attic with a headlamp.

The Cause Nobody Discusses: The Death Spiral

High turnover creates a self-reinforcing cycle that accelerates the problem. When experienced technicians leave, service quality drops. Callbacks increase. Customer satisfaction declines. Revenue falls. The business cuts costs, which means fewer tools, slower parts procurement, and more pressure on the remaining techs. The remaining techs see their workload increase and their support decrease. They leave. Quality drops further.

I have watched operations enter this spiral. It usually takes 18 to 24 months to go from "mild turnover problem" to "existential staffing crisis." By the time most business owners recognize the pattern, rebuilding the team requires 12 months and a significant investment that they no longer have the cash flow to make.

What Actually Reverses the Numbers

The encouraging finding in our data is that the solutions are not mysterious. Operations using platforms designed around technician needs report dramatically different retention numbers. Across our customer base, annual turnover runs between 18% and 24%, roughly a third of the industry average.

The common elements:

  • Full earnings transparency. No take rate. When a technician completes a $400 job, $400 shows up in their ledger. No hidden deductions, no confusing fee schedules. The customer pays the business, the business pays the technician, the platform charges a flat subscription. Clean.
  • Skill-based routing. Jobs matched to certifications, experience, and stated preferences. If a technician holds a master electrician license with commercial experience, they get commercial electrical work. Not residential smoke detector installs.
  • Tools that work in the real world. Offline-capable mobile apps that function in basements and mechanical rooms. Photo documentation that queues locally and uploads when signal returns. Voice-to-text job notes so nobody is typing on a phone screen while standing on a ladder. The goal is to cut daily admin from 47 minutes to under 10.
  • Career visibility. Built-in certification tracking, training module access, and clear progression from apprentice to journeyman to master to supervisor. Technicians stay in trades where they can see a future. They leave trades that feel like dead ends.
  • Professional recognition. Technician profiles that display licenses, certifications, specializations, and years of experience. Customer matching based on expertise rather than proximity. Ratings systems that account for job complexity and context.

The ROI of Keeping Your People

Reducing turnover from 67% to 25% on a 50-person team eliminates approximately 21 replacement cycles per year. At $5,000 to $7,000 per replacement, that is $105,000 to $147,000 in direct savings.

But the direct savings are the smaller number. The compounding effects are where the real value lives. Experienced technicians complete jobs 30% faster than new hires during the first-year ramp. They generate 40% fewer callbacks. Their customer satisfaction scores run 25 to 35 points higher on NPS. Those downstream improvements drive revenue growth that dwarfs the direct cost savings.

Better retention leads to better reviews. Better reviews attract better customers. Better customers pay higher rates. Higher rates attract better technicians. The flywheel turns in the right direction.

Or it turns in the wrong direction. 67% annual turnover. Rising replacement costs. Declining service quality. Customer churn. Revenue contraction. More cost cuts. More turnover.

The flywheel does not care which direction you point it. It just accelerates.
